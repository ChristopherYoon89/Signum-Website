User Guidelines

Welcome to the platform! This application is designed to help you discover, organize and monitor news and other content from thousands of sources on one platform. Our system extracts raw data from the web, processes the raw data, classifies articles into topical categories and extracts relevant tags and keywords.

This guide explains the core features and how to use them effectively.

Browsing and Filtering Content by Categories

The simplest way to use the platform is by browsing categorized content. Articles are automatically classified into topical categories based on their content. These categories allow you to quickly filter the overall news stream and focus on areas that matter most to you.

On the Signum News start page, users can scroll to get an overview of the latest news across all categories. At the top of the page, users can open a list of all categories, select one and quickly navigate to the corresponding section. Each category section also includes an upward arrow button that allows users to quickly jump back to the category list at any time.

To explore a specific category in more detail, users can either click "Read more" at the bottom of a category section or select "Articles" from the right-hand sidebar. Both options redirect the user to the dashboard's Articles section, where the full list of articles is available.

The start page is ideal for users who want a quick overview of major developments without setting up detailed preferences. For example, you might choose to browse only Money & Finance and Technology to reduce noise and focus on relevant coverage.

In some cases, articles may overlap multiple categories. To handle this, our system assigns both a primary and a secondary category. The start page displays only the primary category, while the Articles section in the dashboard shows both the primary and secondary classifications.

Politics

The seven geographical categories primarily focus on political news. The Worldwide category mainly, but not exclusively, covers global politics. This includes international organizations, events involving multilateral cooperation across continents, major bilateral relations and topics not confined to a specific region. Occasionally, the category Worldwide also covers events in countries outside the listed regions, such as major developments in Australia, Canada or Antarctica.

The other geographical categories focus on domestic politics within their respective regions or on bi- and multilateral issues primarily involving those areas. It is important to note that the classification of regions is not solely based on geography but also considers cultural aspects. For example, the Middle East category includes Arab countries in North Africa as well as countries in West Asia, such as Iran.

Economics

The Economics cluster includes the categories Money & Finance, Companies, Commodities and Bitcoin. Money & Finance covers monetary topics, financial developments and, in some cases, broader economic trends. This category typically focuses on macroeconomic perspectives. The "Companies" category primarily includes articles about specific companies, but it may also cover topics related to management, entrepreneurship, marketing or entire industries. Commodities addresses topics such as oil, gas, coal, industrial metals, agricultural products, livestock and rare earths. Bitcoin focuses on BTC while occasionally covering broader cryptocurrency or blockchain topics. Articles about cryptocurrencies other than Bitcoin are not published at all or only in rare cases.

Society

The Society cluster includes Technology, Science, Culture and Panorama. Technology covers a wide range of tech-related news, including software and apps, hardware, cybersecurity and hacking and engineering developments. Science primarily focuses on scientific discoveries, research developments and academic debates. Culture encompasses social norms and values, art, film, music and occasionally philosophical discussions. The category Panorama is inspired by the German media landscape, where the term refers to general news and everyday events. This includes crime, extreme weather, disasters, human-interest stories, sports and unusual or odd news.

Custom Feeds

One of the most important features of Signum News is the custom feeds, which allow users to define their own algorithm and create highly targeted information streams. Custom feeds are displayed in the dashboard under the "Briefing" section, which can be accessed either via the quick-access sidebar at the right or through the dashboard side menu.

When a user creates an account, Signum automatically generates three default feeds that appear in the Briefing: All Articles, My Bookmarks and My Sources. These feeds cannot be deleted. However, users can remove them from the Briefing view via the Settings page.

On the "Add Feed" page, users can create new feeds and customize their filters and algorithm to match specific interests. Each feed can be configured using the following parameters:

  • Feed name
  • Included or excluded categories
  • Included or excluded keywords
  • Included or excluded sources
  • Minimum user rating
  • Minimum algorithm rating
  • Minimum source rating
  • Minimum number of clicks

On the "Edit Feed" page, users can modify previously created feeds and adjust all of the parameters listed above. In the "Settings" page, users can also reorder their feeds and control how they appear on the "Briefing" page.

Since Signum News is still in its infancy, we currently do not support AND/OR operators, but we plan to introduce more advanced query logic in the future. At present, all filters are combined using AND logic. For example, if an article contains an included keyword but belongs to an excluded category, it will not appear in the feed.

Examples

Let us assume a user wants to follow political developments in Western countries. To do so, the user can include the categories USA and Europe. At the same time, the user may wish to avoid saturated topics such as the war in Ukraine. This can be achieved by excluding specific keywords such as "Ukraine", "Zelensky", or "Donbas". Additionally, the user can include or exclude certain sources or define minimum rating requirements. This configuration would surface political developments in the West while filtering out topics the user no longer wishes to follow.

Another example would be a feed dedicated to developments related to Israel and its conflicts. In this case, the user could define keywords such as "Israel", "Netanyahu" and "Hamas" to filter news focused on the respective topic. Furthermore, the user could exclude sources that are considered unreliable on this issue or that exhibit a perceived bias.

Bookmark Lists for Research

Bookmark lists allow you to save articles into structured collections. These lists are designed for research, tracking topics over time or organizing reading material. Instead of losing important articles in a general bookmark section, you can group them by topic and return to them later. This makes bookmark lists particularly useful for deep research and ongoing monitoring.

These bookmark lists allow users to create multiple bookmark lists, name each list based on a topic or project, add articles from any feed or category and build long-term research collections. For instance, user might create bookmark lists such as "AI regulation research", "crude oil market" or "Epstein research".

The customized lists are available in the "Bookmarks" section of your dashboard. You can create and edit bookmark lists via the top menu. To add an article to a specific list, click the star icon on the article and select the desired bookmark list.

Following Sources

If you regularly read content from specific publishers, you can follow sources directly. Following a source prioritizes its articles in your feeds and allows you to easily monitor output from trusted outlets. To follow a particular source, you can click on the + symbol next to a source. You can also search for a particular source and follow it in the "Sources" section in the Dashboard side menu.

This is particularly useful when users trust certain outlets more than others, want to track certain niche industry sources or are interested in following specific regional media outlets. Moreover, the feature also allows users to compare coverage across different publishers. If you want more than one following list, you can create different feeds and include specific sources for each individual feed.

Article Ratings

Users can rate articles on a scale from 1 to 5, which allows us to receive user feedback. Ratings help us evaluate content quality and also contribute to personalization. Over time, ratings can be used as a signal for sorting and prioritizing content in your feeds.

Individual user ratings cannot be viewed by the public, but Signum calculates averages across all user ratings for individual articles. Moreover, user ratings are used to calculate an average user rating of sources in addition to our internal algorithm rating of our data pipeline.

Stats

Users can click the graph icon on any article to view a variety of metrics for both the article and its source, including:

  • Individual user rating
  • Average user rating
  • Algorithm rating
  • Article clicks
  • Average user rating of the source
  • Average algorithm rating of the source

We recommend that users rate articles only after reading them and based on their quality. Ratings can be updated at any time, which will adjust the article's average user rating. This average represents the aggregated rating from all users for that specific article.

The algorithm rating is generated by our internal system and is based on automated calculations. The ranking score considers multiple dimensions, including content quality, topic relevance and the average user rating of the source. Our algorithm rating of the content quality is mainly taking quantitative factors into account, such as the length of the article or the information density.

The article clicks metric represents the total number of unique users who clicked the link; multiple clicks by the same user are not counted. The average user rating of a source reflects the aggregated user ratings across all articles from that source, while the average algorithmic rating of a source is calculated from the algorithmic ratings of all of its articles.
